Overview

What this project set out to answer

What are the key issues at the Manlius Town Hall and Police Station, how should they be prioritized, what options exist for addressing them, and how can they be communicated transparently to the community?

Town staff have long known of facility issues across both buildings. In response, the Town Board initiated a community engagement process and convened a study group of community representatives. Our Maxwell team was brought on to compile and refine that information into a clear, data-driven set of findings and options the community can weigh.

Our approach

How we identified and addressed the issues

We grounded the work in the community's own priorities, then built outward through site visits, peer comparison, legal research, and cost analysis.

  • Gathered community information and reviewed engagement feedback and supplemental documents
  • Toured the Manlius Town Hall and Police Department, plus the DeWitt and Camillus town halls for comparison
  • Held a community listening session on June 8th with a focus group of study-group applicants
  • Researched the legal requirements governing the facilities
  • Categorized and prioritized the concerns we found
  • Developed a set of options and ran a cost-benefit analysis on each

What the community told us

The listening session shaped everything downstream. Residents asked for a clear list of problems, insisted that transparency was essential, and named safety and ADA accessibility as top priorities. They wanted decisions to be data-driven, were wary of tax increases, and asked that the facilities be made functional rather than impressive. Many also wanted to see how Manlius compares to neighboring towns.

Findings

Where Manlius stands

Compared with its neighbors, Manlius is the largest town by both population and area, and has the highest median household income — yet it carries the second-highest tax base, the third-highest budget, and the lowest property tax revenue. In short: larger and wealthier, but facing greater problems with fewer resources.

Peer towns set a useful bar. DeWitt operates with a larger staff and stronger security — bulletproof glass and doors, interconnected private workspace, and full ADA compliance. Camillus has a designated buildings supervisor, an electronic lock and badging system, and is actively investing in ADA compliance such as an elevator retrofit.

The 32 issues, organized

We sorted every issue across both buildings into four categories — Accessibility, Security/Safety, Operational, and Electrical — and three priority tiers: Legally Required, Should Fix, and Ideally Fix. That structure lets the Town see at a glance what it must do, what it should do, and what it could do.

The options

Five paths forward

Each option packages the issues differently, trading up-front cost against how completely the facilities' problems get solved. The cost-benefit analysis details the financials behind each.

A
Do MinimumFix only the urgent problems in both existing buildings.
B
Full RebuildOne new combined building for Town Hall, Police, and Court.
C
Rebuild Police + Renovate Town HallBuild a new Police/Court building; fully renovate the Town Hall.
D
Purchase Police + Renovate Town HallBuy and convert a building for Police and Court; fully renovate the Town Hall.
E
Purchase Police + Do-Minimum Town HallBuy and convert for Police and Court, with only urgent repairs to the Town Hall.
